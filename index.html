About

"I became a journalist to find the right words. I became an academic to understand who decides which words are allowed."

I am Misbahul Haque ‚Äî a journalist-turned-academic from Dhaka, Bangladesh, now based in the United States. My career has been built on the conviction that ideas, rigorously argued, can change how people think ‚Äî and that the laws governing the press shape what ideas are allowed into public life at all.

I spent most of my professional years in journalism, doing the slow, deliberate work of journalism ‚Äî writing pieces that gave readers not just what happened, but what it means. I wrote about politics, media, religion, society, and culture for some of Bangladesh's most read publications.

That career spanned Daily Manabzamin, United News of Bangladesh (UNB), The Business Standard, and Prothom Alo ‚Äî Bangladesh's largest circulating daily. I also contributed to The Diplomat, covering the Asia-Pacific for an international audience, and to the Columbia Missourian here in the United States.

The questions that drove my journalism ‚Äî about power, press freedom, and the boundaries of public discourse ‚Äî eventually pulled me toward academic research. I wanted to understand not just what the law says journalists can do, but why, and whose interests those rules protect. That inquiry brought me to the Missouri School of Journalism at the University of Missouri, where I am pursuing a Master's in Journalism with a focus on media and communication law.

My academic foundation is in the humanities: I hold a Bachelor's and a Master's in World Religions and Culture from the University of Dhaka. That training ‚Äî in how belief systems shape human societies and how narratives construct meaning ‚Äî informs the way I approach both journalism and media scholarship.
